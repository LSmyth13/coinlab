Follow-up — stablecoin mechanics
How does it
stay stable?
All stablecoins want to be worth $1. They get there in completely different ways — and they fail in completely different ways too. This distinction matters enormously.
🏦
Fiat-backed — real dollars in a real bank
1:1 backed by actual USD (or GBP, EUR) held in a bank or money market fund. Simple, auditable, and trusted. You hold the dollars and issue tokens against them. USDC publishes monthly reserve attestations. The boring choice is usually the right choice.
Lowest risk — boring is beautiful
⛓️
Crypto-collateralised — backed by other crypto
Over-collateralised — deposit $150 of ETH to mint $100 of stablecoin — to absorb price swings. Fully on-chain, no banks required, genuinely decentralised. MakerDAO's DAI has maintained its peg through multiple 80%+ crypto crashes using this model since 2017.
Medium risk — clever but complex
🤖
Algorithmic — backed by maths and prayers
No collateral at all. Smart contracts mint and burn tokens to maintain the peg. Elegant in theory, capital-efficient on paper. In May 2022, Terra/LUNA lost $40 billion in 72 hours and triggered a global crypto contagion. We mention this for educational purposes.
Very high risk — ask Terra how it went
→ Pick a stability mechanism to continue.
Step 2 of 3 — supply model
How many coins
will ever exist?
This single decision shapes everything: scarcity, inflation, staking rewards, and whether your coin feels like digital gold or a supermarket loyalty card.
🔒
Fixed cap — like Bitcoin's 21 million
Hard limit forever. Scarcity is structural and permanent. As demand grows and supply can't respond, price does all the adjusting. Beloved by long-term holders and Austrian economists everywhere.
♾️
Inflationary — new coins can always be minted
Useful for staking rewards, liquidity mining incentives, and ongoing development funding. Most DeFi tokens use this model. Ethereum partially offsets inflation by burning transaction fees. Requires someone sensible controlling the printer.
📌
Elastic — supply adjusts to hit a price target
Expands and contracts automatically to maintain a target price. Required plumbing for algorithmic stablecoins. Works smoothly in calm, liquid markets — and can catastrophically accelerate the spiral it was designed to stop when markets panic.
→ Pick a supply model to continue.
Step 3 — who's in charge
Who controls
this thing?
Every coin needs someone — or something — making decisions. Who gets the power, and who gets the blame when it all goes sideways?
🏢
A company
One organisation makes all decisions. Efficient, fast, accountable. Also the exact thing crypto was invented to eliminate — a single centralised point of trust and failure.
🌐
A DAO (token holders vote)
Decentralised governance — every major decision goes to a community vote. In practice: participation under 5%, and one wallet with 60% of the supply.
👤
The founders (for now)
A small founding team runs things until the project is "sufficiently decentralised." A common approach — and a phrase that has done a lot of heavy lifting in regulatory conversations.
🤖
Nobody — smart contracts only
Code runs everything automatically. Rules are set at launch, no human can override them. Maximum trustlessness. Also: if there's a bug, nobody can fix it.
→ Someone has to take the blame. Pick one.
